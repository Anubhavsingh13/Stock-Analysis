## Executive Summary

Chembond Chemicals is the demerged water-treatment and construction-chemicals arm of the old Chembond group — a debt-free, net-cash, 23.6% ROCE specialty chemicals business where recurring industrial water-treatment consumables and services (87% of revenue) provide genuine customer stickiness. **The business quality is real; the price no longer reflects it.** The stock has already re-rated 142% off its post-demerger forced-selling trough of ₹104.85 to ₹254, and at that level my base-case DCF (₹228) sits *below* the market price, with an upside/downside of roughly 0.5:1. The thesis is that the demerger-arbitrage window — the actual opportunity here — **has already closed**; the key risk to any position taken now is paying a full price for a company with only one year of standalone financials and 128 debtor days.

## BUSINESS PRIMER

Chembond Chemicals sells industrial specialty chemicals and the equipment and services that go with them, overwhelmingly to other businesses rather than consumers. Its dominant vertical — roughly 87% of revenue — is **water technologies**: chemicals that treat boiler water, cooling towers, and effluent for industrial plants, sold alongside dosing equipment and on-site service contracts. Revenue flows as volume × realisation on a consumable that the customer must repurchase continuously to keep its plant running, supplemented by lumpier equipment orders (the water business unit carried a >₹100 crore order book entering FY27). Smaller verticals are **construction chemicals** (admixtures, waterproofing — ₹13cr in H2FY26) and a **distribution** arm. Operations are India-centric, run out of the legacy Chembond manufacturing base. The two things this business absolutely depends on are industrial capacity utilisation among its customers (no plant running = no water to treat) and its ability to hold formulation pricing against a fragmented competitive field.

This is a **capital-light B2B specialty chemicals compounder with a services overlay** — not a commodity chemical producer. The key value driver is volume growth in the recurring consumables base, with mix (higher-value formulations, equipment attach) as the margin lever; realisation is a secondary driver because industrial water chemicals are specified into plant operating protocols rather than tendered on price each cycle. The key risk is customer-side industrial capex and utilisation cyclicality, plus the fragmentation of the water-treatment field (Ion Exchange, Thermax, and global majors like Ecolab/Nalco and Veolia all compete here). **The structural feature that colours this entire analysis is the demerger itself**: with only ~2 years of carved-out financials and ~1 year of listed price history, every trend statement in this report rests on a thin base, and the corporate-cost allocations between the two demerged entities have not yet been through a full cycle of audited comparability.

## MODULE 3 — Business Quality & Moat

### 3A. Greenwald's Three Barriers — Tested Explicitly

**1. Supply / Cost Advantage — ✗ FAIL.** No evidence Chembond makes water-treatment chemicals more cheaply than Ion Exchange or Ecolab. There is no captive feedstock, no scale advantage (₹326cr revenue vs. Ecolab's global scale), no process IP disclosed. A funded entrant *can* match this cost structure by spending money.

**2. Customer Captivity — ✓ PASS (the real moat, and it is narrow).** This is the one barrier that holds. Industrial water treatment is **specified into the plant's operating protocol**: the chemistry is validated against that specific boiler/cooling system, the dosing equipment is Chembond's, and the service engineer knows the plant. Switching means re-validation, re-qualification, and downtime risk on a system whose failure shuts the plant. The 87%-of-revenue recurring consumables base is the evidence. **But note the limit:** if captivity were strong, realisation would be rising. It is falling (~−9% implied in H2FY26). **Customers are captive enough not to leave, but not captive enough to accept price increases.**

**3. Local Economies of Scale — ✗ FAIL.** No evidence of >50% share in any bounded geography or product niche. Chembond is a mid-sized player in a fragmented national market.

**Moat Verdict: NARROW.** Source: customer captivity via switching costs. One of three barriers.

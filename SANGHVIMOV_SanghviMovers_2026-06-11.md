## Executive Summary

Sanghvi Movers is India's largest crane rental company and the world's fifth largest (International Cranes, June 2025), operating 500+ cranes in a near-monopoly niche for large hydraulic lifts (>200T). The company delivered a record FY26 with revenue of ₹1,070 Cr (+37% YoY), EBITDA margin of ~40%, and a ₹1,053 Cr order book, supported by India's record 6.05 GW wind addition in FY26 and an accelerating industrial capex cycle. At the current price of ₹372 (P/E ~17x, EV/EBITDA ~9x), the stock is close to fair value; a 10–15% pullback to ₹310–330 would offer a meaningful margin of safety for a 2–3 year hold targeting ₹520–580 (base case).

## Module 2 — Industry Structure & Capital Cycle / Business Quality & Moat

### Porter Five Forces: Large Crane Rental (>200T) in India

**1. Threat of New Entrants: VERY LOW**
- A 200T–1600T crane costs ₹5–50 Cr per unit; building a fleet of 50+ large cranes requires ₹500–2,500 Cr of capital
- Procurement lead times: 18–24 months (European manufacturers: Liebherr, Tadano, Manitowoc, Grove)
- Specialised operator training: 3–5 years to develop skilled crews; safety regulations stringent
- Customer relationships for multi-year crane rental contracts require track record and financial stability
- Sanghvi's 30+ year relationship base and 15 depots across India are near-impossible to replicate quickly
- **Conclusion: Near-dominant moat in >200T segment**

**2. Bargaining Power of Buyers: MODERATE**
- Buyers (wind turbine OEMs like Vestas/Siemens Gamesa, refineries, power cos) are sophisticated
- For >400T lifts, Sanghvi is often the ONLY option in India → near-zero buyer power in that sub-segment
- For 200–400T: moderate competitive tension with smaller regional players; some price sensitivity
- Long-term contracts (3–5 year crane fleet reservations for large wind projects) reduce short-term buyer power

**3. Bargaining Power of Suppliers: LOW-MODERATE**
- Crane OEMs (Liebherr, Tadano) have pricing power for new equipment — lead times and prices are rising
- But Sanghvi's scale gives it better terms than smaller players; it buys 20–50 cranes per capex cycle
- Diesel suppliers: pure commodity, no meaningful bargaining power

**4. Threat of Substitutes: VERY LOW**
- No credible substitute for large hydraulic cranes in wind turbine erection or refinery module lifts
- No technology disruption horizon visible; physics of heavy lifting remains unchanged

**5. Competitive Rivalry: LOW (in large crane segment)**
- National competitor with >200T fleet: essentially none at Sanghvi's scale
- Sarens (Belgian MNC): present in India but smaller fleet, different cost structure
- ACE (Action Construction Equipment): dominates <400T, manufacturing-oriented, different model
- Regional players: exist in <150T; not directly competitive with Sanghvi's core fleet

**Industry Structure Verdict:** VERY ATTRACTIVE — near-monopoly economics in core niche

### Capital Cycle Analysis
The crane rental industry is at an unusual point: demand (wind energy pipeline) is accelerating simultaneously with the need for fleet expansion. Sanghvi is spending ₹629 Cr in FY26 on fleet additions — this creates a further moat widening (more cranes = more job-sites served = more entrenchment of customer relationships). Competitors who don't invest now risk being permanently capacity-constrained. The capital cycle is in Sanghvi's favour as the dominant incumbent.

### Business Quality & Moat: Greenwald's 3 Barriers

**1. Supply-side advantages (Production Advantages):** STRONG
- Scale: 500+ cranes across 15 depots gives operating leverage competitors cannot match
- Operational expertise: 30 years of specialised crane operation, trained workforce
- Proprietary operational know-how in complex lifts (e.g., 1,600T capacity lifts)

**2. Demand-side advantages (Customer Captivity):** MODERATE-STRONG
- Switching costs: Wind/refinery project managers prefer proven crane operators; switching mid-project is operationally infeasible
- Long-term contracts (3–5 year reservations for wind energy projects) create revenue visibility
- Reputation is a genuine asset in a business where a crane failure can be catastrophic

**3. Economies of scale:** STRONG
- 500+ crane fleet = near-constant fixed costs (depot infrastructure, IT, HR) spread across larger base
- Incremental cranes have much lower overhead than standalone operators

### Moat Durability 5 Tests
1. **Is the moat wide?** Yes — effectively monopoly in >400T large crawler crane segment in India
2. **Is it widening?** Yes — fleet expansion deepens scale advantage
3. **Is it durable?** Yes — high capital barriers make disruption 10+ year horizon
4. **What could erode it?** A well-capitalised foreign entrant (Sarens, Mammoet) aggressively expanding in India; policy disruption to wind energy; tech disruption (not visible)
5. **Is management nurturing it?** Yes — international expansion (KSA, Botswana) shows ambition to leverage core moat

**Moat Assessment: NARROW-TO-WIDE (tending wider)**

## Business Primer

Ellenbarrie Industrial Gases Ltd (EIGL) manufactures and supplies industrial and medical gases — oxygen, nitrogen, argon, hydrogen, helium, acetylene, CO₂, nitrous oxide, dry ice, LPG, specialty/welding gas mixtures and synthetic air — to industrial, healthcare and defence customers across eastern and southern India. It is the largest 100%-Indian-owned industrial gases company by installed capacity, with over 50 years of operating history, and is the market leader in West Bengal, Andhra Pradesh and Telangana. Revenue comes through three channels: Bulk/merchant sales (cryogenic liquid delivered by tanker, ~67% of FY25 revenue), Packaged/cylinder gas (~18%), and Onsite (a captive air separation unit built inside a customer's plant under a long-term supply contract, ~16%, plus ~6% from project engineering services). Customers span steel (Tata Steel, Tata Metaliks), shipbuilding, glass, pharma, fabrication/welding, water treatment and hospitals — with a genuinely diversified base of over 1,800 customers in FY24, limiting single-customer concentration risk. The business absolutely depends on two things: continuous, reliable air separation unit (ASU) uptime (this is a 24x7 process business — any plant outage directly hits revenue) and proximity-driven regional density (liquid gas cannot be economically trucked far, so market leadership is inherently local/regional, not national).

This is a capital-intensive, quasi-utility-like manufacturing business: an asset-heavy compounder riding two tailwinds simultaneously — India's steel-capacity buildout (oxygen is a core steel-making input) and rising medical/industrial gas penetration. It just IPO'd (July 2025) and is mid-way through an aggressive, debt-funded-turned-equity-funded capacity expansion (roughly doubling installed capacity from ~1,370 TPD at FY25-end to a targeted ~2,130 TPD by FY27-end). The key value drivers are volume (new ASU capacity coming online) and mix (rising argon/specialty-gas share lifting margins) rather than pricing power in bulk gas, which is largely cost-plus/contracted. The key risks are: execution risk on a large multi-plant capex programme (already showing schedule slippage — the North India plant delayed from 2QFY27 to 2HFY27), customer/end-market concentration in steel-sector capex cycles, and a structural feature that colors this entire analysis — the stock is down over 50% from its listing-day market cap of ₹7,534 crore to ~₹3,700–3,850 crore within 12 months, meaning a very rich IPO/listing-day valuation is normalizing hard even as the underlying business executes reasonably on plan. Any analysis here must separate "good business, wrong IPO price" from "business problem."

## Module 1b — Sector Cycle: Tailwinds & Headwinds

**Secular vs. Cyclical Demand Decomposition:** Ellenbarrie's 5-year sales CAGR of ~14% (FY20-FY25/26) is a blend of: (a) secular — India's steel capacity build-out (government target ~20% CAGR in steel capacity through 2030 per RHP), rising healthcare/medical-gas penetration, water/wastewater treatment (Jal Jeevan Mission), and industrial gases market growing ~6.4% CAGR structurally; and (b) cyclical/company-specific — a large one-off capacity-addition cycle (Kurnool, Uluberia-II, TML Kharagpur) that is inflating growth well above the underlying market's ~6-8% structural rate. FY25/26 revenue growth of ~9-14% in the "core gases" segment is largely volume from new capacity ramping, not a demand-side cyclical spike. Estimate: roughly 40-50% of recent growth is secular (steel/medical gas structural demand), 50-60% is company-specific capacity-addition (a one-time step-change, not a repeatable growth rate once the current capex cycle completes around FY27-28). **This should NOT be extrapolated at 20-25% CAGR indefinitely** (which is management's stated long-term target) — durable structural baseline is closer to 10-14%.

**Input cost cycle:** Power (electricity) is the dominant input for ASUs — largely stable/regulated in India, modest upward drift. Argon prices are globally volatile and a meaningful margin swing factor (management cited "recovering argon prices" as an FY26 tailwind).

**Competitive capacity pipeline:** This is the single most important sector-cycle fact. Per the company's own RHP, **every major competitor is simultaneously expanding capacity**: Linde India (~$1bn planned, new Kalinganagar ASU ₹425cr, SAIL expansion), Inox Air Products (~$360mn by 2025, 10 new ASUs, Tata Steel Meramandali contract, BPCL Kochi), Air Liquide, Air Water, Yingde Gases (~$1bn planned). This is a textbook Chancellor capital-cycle signal: **simultaneous multi-player capacity expansion in a 2-4 year window → structural oversupply risk and pricing/margin pressure**, independent of underlying steel/medical demand growth. This tempers the bull case on margin expansion.

**Sector cycle verdict: Neutral-to-Headwind.** Primary driver: structural demand (steel, medical) is real and durable, but the capital cycle shows broad-based simultaneous capacity additions across all major players — a classic late-stage capital cycle signal that argues for caution on sustained pricing power/margin expansion, and for treating current elevated 34-38% EBITDA margins as closer to a cycle peak than a new steady state.

## Module 1c — Stock Cycle: Company-Specific Positioning

**Revenue growth trajectory:** Q1 FY26 +24% YoY, but full-year FY26 revenue growth moderated to +9.3% (₹342cr from ₹312cr), with Q2 FY26 EBITDA actually down 8% YoY (one-off prior-year revenue base effect per Motilal Oswal). This is a **decelerating-into-volatile** pattern quarter to quarter — not a clean acceleration story — even though full-year commentary (20-25% long-term CAGR target) remains bullish.

**Revenue decomposition:** Predominantly volume-driven (new ASU capacity commissioning: Kurnool 600 TPD, Uluberia-II 220 TPD, TML Kharagpur ~250-400 TPD), with a secondary mix tailwind from rising argon/specialty gas share. Pricing in the bulk/onsite segment is largely cost-plus/contracted, so pricing is the least important and least durable driver here — consistent with the framework's warning that price is "first surrendered in competition."

**Physical Capacity Model:** Capacity target trajectory disclosed by management: **FY25-end 1,370 TPD → FY26-end target 1,910 TPD → FY27-end target 2,130 TPD** (owned + operated). Combined Bulk (911 TPD) + Onsite (698 TPD) = ~1,609 TPD as of the Q4 FY26 call, targeting Bulk 1,131 TPD and Onsite ~1,018 TPD (~2,149 TPD) over the next 12 months. Standard ramp curve applies: Kurnool (greenfield/leased, commissioned ~1 year ago) now at 87% utilization — a genuinely strong ramp, ahead of the typical Year-1 25-35% greenfield curve, likely because it operates as a leased/O&M arrangement with Jairaj rather than a pure greenfield build. Uluberia-II (220 TPD, just commissioned) is guided to reach 85% utilization within 18 months — in line with brownfield/expansion norms. **Capacity ceiling:** if all announced plants complete and ramp on schedule, utilization would approach 85-90%+ around FY28, at which point growth must come from a new, currently unannounced capex phase (management has signaled intent but nothing formally announced beyond FY27 targets).

**Operating Leverage:** OPM has expanded sharply from 9% (FY15) to 34-38% (FY25/26) — indicating strong operating leverage as utilization has risen, but this also means the business is now closer to peak margin than trough, with less room for further multiple-driven re-rating from margin expansion alone.

**Management Guidance Accuracy (WTT):** Limited track record (only ~4 quarters of public guidance since IPO). One clear miss already: **North India plant commissioning delayed from 2QFY27 to 2HFY27**, causing Motilal Oswal to cut FY27/FY28 EPS estimates by 13%/9%. This is a one-quarter-plus delay within the very first year of being a listed company — a caution flag on execution reliability for a business whose entire investment case rests on capex delivery timing. **WTT Score (preliminary, small sample): ~0.5-0.6 (MODERATE-LOW)** — haircut all forward capacity/commissioning guidance by 10-20% (add 1-2 quarters of delay) pending a longer track record.

**FCF Inflection Year:** Currently negative FCF (-₹25cr in FY26, -₹65cr in FY24) due to heavy ongoing capex. Given the FY27-28 capacity buildout is still underway, FCF inflection is not expected before **FY28-29** at the earliest, once the current multi-plant capex cycle matures and working capital stabilizes.

**Stock Cycle Classification: Early-cycle / Ramp-up.** Capex-heavy, margins near-peak-but-volatile, revenue growth lumpy quarter to quarter as new plants come online in a step-function (not smooth). Standard MoS applies; position size should stay modest until at least 2-3 more quarters of guidance-delivery track record accumulate.

## Module 3 — Business Quality & Moat

**Greenwald's Three Barriers:**
1. **Supply/Cost Advantage:** Partial — Ellenbarrie's regional density (multiple plants across a concentrated geography) gives it a logistics/cost edge over a national player trying to serve the same region from a farther plant, since liquid gas transport radius is limited (~200-300km economically). This is real but geographically bounded, not a company-wide moat.
2. **Customer Captivity:** Moderate — Onsite contracts (long-term, in-plant ASUs, e.g., TML Kharagpur, Kurnool/Jairaj) create genuine switching costs (15-year agreements, integrated into customer's own production line). Bulk/Package segments have low captivity — largely contract-renewal/price-driven.
3. **Local Economies of Scale:** Yes — Ellenbarrie is the explicit market leader in West Bengal, Andhra Pradesh and Telangana. This is the strongest and most defensible moat claim, consistent with the "local economies of scale" pattern (similar to Star Cement in the Northeast).

**Moat Verdict: Narrow.** Real but geographically bounded — a strong regional moat in 3 states, not a national or pricing-power moat. Buffett's 10%-price-increase test likely fails for Bulk/Package (commodity, contract-driven) but may partially pass for long-term Onsite contracts already signed.

**Fisher 15-Point Checklist (abbreviated, key points):**
- Market potential: Good (steel capex cycle, medical gas penetration) — score 3
- New product/geography expansion: Yes, actively expanding into new states (North India plant planned) — score 2-3
- Margin trend: Improving sharply (9%→38% OPM over a decade) — score 3
- Management depth: Limited public track record post-IPO; family-promoter-led (77% holding) — score 2
- Point 15 (Integrity): No disclosed SFIO/SEBI enforcement issues found in this research; but note the company has **zero dividend payout** despite consistent profitability for years pre-IPO — not a veto, but noted as a capital-allocation/shareholder-orientation question, not an integrity failure. **No veto triggered.**

**Estimated Fisher Score: ~30-33/45** (solid but not exceptional — moderate management depth visibility is the main drag, being a recently-listed company).

## Module 6 — Earnings Quality & DuPont

**ROIC:** ROCE has been volatile and declining — 53% (FY20, likely distorted by a small capital base) → 18% (FY25) → 15% (FY26, 3-yr avg 12%). This declining trend, even as the business has scaled, is a **DuPont-relevant signal**: capital employed (from the capex programme) is growing faster than operating returns on that capital, at least in the ramp-up phase. This is expected during a capacity build-out (new assets not yet at steady-state utilization drag down blended ROCE) but should be monitored — if ROCE keeps declining after FY27-28 (post-ramp), that would indicate the new capacity is not earning adequate returns.

**ROE:** 14% (3-yr avg), roughly in line with ROCE, suggesting leverage is not artificially propping up ROE — a clean signal (financing contribution is not masking declining operations, per the DuPont diagnostic).

**Economic Profit:** With ROCE (12-18%) likely above India WACC (~11-13% for a small-cap industrial manufacturer with moderate leverage), the business is still value-creating on a blended basis, but the spread has compressed meaningfully from FY20 levels — consistent with the "declining ROIC over 3+ years" moat-erosion watch signal, though here it's more plausibly cycle/ramp-related than structural moat erosion, given the ongoing capex programme explains most of the capital-base growth.

**Earnings Quality Verdict:** Aggressive but defensible — the FY25 other-income inflation and negative FCF are real concerns but explainable by the capex cycle rather than indicating manipulation. ## Module 8 — Market Size & Market Share

**TAM:** Indian industrial gases market ~US$1.45 billion (2025) projected to reach ~US$2.24 billion by 2032 (6.41% CAGR) — a modest-growth structural market. Steel-linked oxygen demand and medical/healthcare gas demand are the two strongest sub-segment growth vectors (steel capacity targeted to grow ~20% CAGR through 2030 per government plans cited in the RHP — though this steel-capacity growth number is materially more aggressive than the blended 6.4% industrial gases TAM growth, implying steel-linked oxygen demand should outgrow the overall gases market).

**Market Share:** Ellenbarrie is the largest 100%-Indian-owned player nationally and the explicit market leader in West Bengal, Andhra Pradesh and Telangana — but a small fraction of the national market dominated by Linde, Inox Air Products, Air Liquide and Air Water. Share gain, where it's happening, is structural (new capacity in specific geographies) rather than share taken from incumbents in existing markets — i.e., Ellenbarrie is capturing a slice of *new* capacity build in the industry rather than displacing competitors.

**Verdict:** Growing faster than the overall TAM (14% company CAGR vs. ~6.4% market CAGR) — but this outperformance is substantially a function of Ellenbarrie's own capex-driven capacity additions (i.e., a low base effect) rather than organic like-for-like share gain from competitors. This reconciles with the Module 1b conclusion that much of recent growth is company-specific capacity addition, not demand-side share capture.

## Module 9 — Valuation: PIE First, Then DCF

**Price-Implied Expectations (approximate, given data constraints):**
At a ~₹3,700-3,850cr market cap on ~₹104cr FY26 PAT (P/E ~35-37x) and ~₹342cr revenue, the market is pricing in a multi-year growth and margin story well above the "clean" run-rate. Stripping the ₹50cr FY25 other-income boost, normalized core operating PAT is materially lower than headline PAT — meaning the *effective* P/E on clean operating earnings is higher than the headline 35-37x suggests.

**Expectations Gap:** The current price appears to be betting on: (a) successful, on-time ramp of ~2,130 TPD of capacity by FY27-end, (b) sustained 34-38% EBITDA margins (near-peak, cycle-high levels) even as every major competitor simultaneously adds capacity (Module 1b headwind), and (c) continuation of the 20-25% long-term CAGR management has guided to — a rate that is difficult to reconcile with the ~6.4% TAM growth once the current capex-driven step-change is fully absorbed (by ~FY28-29).

**Physical plausibility check:** Capacity model supports strong growth through FY27-28 (capacity roughly doubling from FY25 to FY27) — this can support the near-term (2-3 year) growth story. But management's stated "20-25% CAGR" as a *durable long-term* rate is not physically supported by any *announced* capacity beyond FY27 — this is either (a) market/management pricing an unannounced next capex phase, or (b) an aspirational long-term target that will need to be revised down once the current capacity plan is fully utilized, absent new announcements.

**SVAR:** Given the already-substantial ~50% correction from listing highs, some of the "optimistic gap" has already been priced out. However, at a still-premium 35-37x P/E (and higher on normalized earnings) versus sector peer median ~21x P/E, further downside exists if FY27-28 execution disappoints (as it already has once, with the North India plant delay) or if margins compress due to sector-wide capacity overhang.

**Margin of Safety:** Given (a) small-cap size, (b) recent IPO with limited public track record, (c) WTT score in the LOW-MODERATE range, and (d) a capital cycle headwind from simultaneous competitor capacity expansion — this situation calls for a **wide margin of safety (40%+ discount to intrinsic value)**. At current pricing (~35-37x trailing P/E, premium to peers), that margin of safety is **not clearly established** — the stock looks more reasonably priced than at listing, but not yet cheap.

**Verdict: MoS Gate — Not clearly passed.** Business quality is reasonable (narrow regional moat, strong margin trajectory, clean promoter pledge), but price still embeds optimistic execution and margin-durability assumptions in a sector facing a capacity-overhang headwind.

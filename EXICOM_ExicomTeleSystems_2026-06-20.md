## Executive Summary

Exicom Tele-Systems is India's largest EV charger manufacturer with a strategic bet on global expansion through the August 2024 acquisition of distressed Australian DC fast charger maker Tritium. The standalone India business (critical power + EVSE) is fundamentally sound and improving, but the Tritium acquisition has loaded the balance sheet with ₹713 crore in debt and generated cumulative consolidated losses of ₹384 crore in FY25–26. At ₹173/share (EV ~₹3,000 crore), the market is pricing the bull case — where Tritium turns profitable by Q4 FY27, BharatNet executes fully, and EVSE grows at 22%+ — as its central scenario. ## Business Primer

**Paragraph 1 — What the business does and how it makes money:**
Exicom Tele-Systems Limited (NSE: EXICOM) is a Gurugram-based power management solutions company operating across two distinct, largely unrelated segments. The Critical Power division designs, manufactures, and services DC power systems, rectifiers, and Li-ion energy storage units for telecom towers and broadband infrastructure — its economics are driven by telecom capex cycles and large government contracts, most recently the ₹1,680 crore BharatNet order (3-year execution, ₹1,461 crore remaining as of December 2025) for supplying DC power and energy storage to rural broadband sites. The EV Charging (EVSE) division manufactures AC wall boxes and DC fast chargers for residential, commercial, fleet, and public charging use; it claims to be India's largest EV charger manufacturer by deployed base (1.73 lakh units globally) and earns revenue through B2B hardware supply to OEMs, CPOs, and government agencies, supplemented by growing service income. In August 2024, the company made a transformative and high-risk acquisition: Tritium, an Australian DC fast charger company with manufacturing in Tennessee, USA, bought out of financial distress for USD ~29.6 million. Tritium adds a third revenue stream targeting global markets across 47 countries but has dragged consolidated financials into deep losses since acquisition. The business's economics depend on two things: (1) telecom/government capex for critical power, and (2) EV penetration velocity and public charging infrastructure deployment for EVSE.

**Paragraph 2 — What kind of business this is and what to watch:**
Exicom is a capital-intensive hardware manufacturer at an early stage of a secular growth cycle — not a capital-light compounder. The Tritium acquisition fundamentally changed the balance sheet: borrowings surged from ₹46 crore in FY24 to ₹707 crore in FY25 as Tritium brought manufacturing assets, accumulated losses, and working capital needs onto the books. Consolidated EBITDA turned deeply negative (-₹37 crore in FY25, -₹103 crore in FY26) while standalone Exicom India remained profitable (PAT ₹13.6 crore in FY26). The key structural risk is the Tritium integration timeline and the pace of turnaround — every quarter Tritium bleeds, it erodes consolidated equity and leverages the balance sheet. The three variables to watch: (1) Tritium quarterly EBITDA — the critical thesis variable; (2) BharatNet order execution + replacement order pipeline — the critical power cliff risk in FY28; (3) consolidated debt trajectory — whether leverage can be reduced without a dilutive equity raise.

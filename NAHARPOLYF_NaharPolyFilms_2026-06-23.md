## Executive Summary

Nahar Poly Films is a small, pure-play BOPP film manufacturer (60,000 MTPA capacity, Mandideep MP) that belongs to the Ludhiana-based Nahar/Oswal Group. The core BOPP business is a structurally unattractive commodity with low returns (average ROIC ~6% over 10 years, below the ~11–12% WACC), no discernible moat, and a sector entering a new overcapacity cycle with peers commissioning large additions. The headline financial improvement in FY25–FY26 is partially misleading: a large investment portfolio (₹611 Cr of investments vs. ₹704 Cr revenue) generates substantial non-operating income, distorting the picture of core operations. The key risk is a planned ₹594 Cr capacity expansion (third BOPP line, 36,000 MTPA) funded primarily via ₹418 Cr of term debt — adding 60% new capacity into an already overcrowded market, which would lever up a balance sheet that is currently clean and nearly debt-free. At a CMP of approximately ₹246–266, the stock trades at ~8× FY26 EPS, which appears cheap, but the investment portfolio (carrying value ₹611 Cr vs. total market cap ~₹600 Cr) means investors are essentially paying operating-business value for free — yet the operating business earns below its cost of capital and faces structural headwinds.

## Business Primer

Nahar Poly Films Limited manufactures biaxially oriented polypropylene (BOPP) films used primarily in flexible packaging — food packaging, textile bags, reverse-printed laminates, industrial tapes, and decorative labels. The company operates two Bruckner BOPP lines totalling 60,000 MTPA at its plant in Mandideep, near Bhopal in Madhya Pradesh. Revenue flows as volume × realization, where realization per MT is the dominant variable driven by global polypropylene (PP) prices and domestic film supply-demand balance. Essentially all revenue is B2B, sold to converters and brand-owners (packagers, FMCG companies, e-commerce). The business absolutely depends on two variables: (1) the spread between BOPP film selling prices and PP resin input cost, and (2) capacity utilisation driving fixed-cost absorption on capital-intensive film lines.

The company is an asset-heavy commodity manufacturer with no meaningful moat. It is part of the seven-decade-old Nahar/Oswal Group (Ludhiana), a diversified conglomerate with interests in textiles (Nahar Spinning Mills, Nahar Industrial Enterprises, Oswal Woollen Mills), retail fashion (Monte Carlo Fashions), and financial services (Nahar Capital and Financial Services). A structural feature that will colour this entire analysis: approximately 61% of Nahar Poly Films' total assets (₹611 Cr of ₹1,000 Cr) sit in investments — including group company stakes, mutual funds, listed equities, and bonds held through Nahar Capital and Financial Services. This investment portfolio generates significant non-operating income and increasingly distorts the reported PAT, making the headline profit figures unreliable as a measure of BOPP manufacturing quality. Promoter holding is concentrated (72.5%) and held primarily through group companies (Nahar Capital 49.2%, Nahar Spinning Mills 18.9%), creating complex related-party interdependencies that must be assessed separately from the core manufacturing business.

## Module 1b — Sector Cycle: BOPP Films

### Secular vs. Cyclical Demand Decomposition

**Secular drivers (durable):**
- India flexible packaging demand tracking nominal GDP + urbanisation + organised retail growth (~8–10% PA structural)
- FMCG premiumisation increasing per-unit film content requirements
- E-commerce secondary packaging growth
- Estimated secular demand component: ~7–8% volume CAGR

**Cyclical drivers (temporary, mean-reverts):**
- Post-COVID restocking of packaging inventory in FY21–FY22 drove the supercycle (revenue jumped from ₹303 Cr in FY21 to ₹489 Cr in FY22 to ₹723 Cr in FY23 — a 139% surge in 2 years)
- The FY23 peak was followed by a sharp correction in FY24 (revenue fell 17% to ₹600 Cr, EBITDA collapsed from ₹82 Cr to ₹21 Cr) — the classic post-overcapacity trough
- FY25–FY26 recovery is a cyclical bounce from trough, not a new structural level

**Verdict:** The 15–20% apparent growth in FY25–FY26 is ~70–80% cyclical (trough recovery) and only ~20–30% secular. Embedding FY26 revenue levels as a permanent baseline would overstate long-run earnings by 20–30%.

### Input Cost Cycle
Polypropylene (PP) pellet prices: broadly stable-to-easing in FY26 (crude oil benign). This is a **modest margin tailwind** in FY26. However, any crude oil spike (+20%) translates to ~8–12% EBITDA margin compression for BOPP manufacturers with a 1–2 quarter lag.

### Inventory Cycle
Industry appears to be in a **mild restocking** phase post the FY23–FY24 destocking trough. Not a significant amplifier in either direction.

### Competitive Capacity Pipeline — CRITICAL CONCERN
This is the most important sector-level risk for Nahar Poly Films:
- **Cosmo Films:** Commissioned new 81,200 MT BOPP line at Aurangabad in June 2025 (+40% to their capacity)
- **Jindal Poly Films:** New Nashik 60,000 MT BOPP plant planned for H2 FY26 (status post-fire at Nashik subsidiary is uncertain but the underlying expansion intent remains)
- **SRF:** 60,000 MT new Indore BOPET line slated for July 2027
- **Toppan Specialty Films:** Hybrid BOPP/BOPE line, +40% capacity, commissioned November 2025
- **Nahar Poly Films itself:** Planning a 36,000 MT third line (₹594 Cr capex)

India's total BOPP installed capacity of ~1.1 million MTPA already shows utilisation in the mid-70s. Simultaneous capacity additions from multiple players in FY26–FY27 risk pushing utilisation below 70%, which historically drives EBITDA/MT toward distress territory (₹3,000–5,000/MT). This is **the most significant sector headwind** for Nahar Poly Films.

### Net FX Exposure
Nahar Poly Films is primarily India-domestic. PP resin input cost is globally referenced (USD-denominated import cost). Export revenue is available but likely small (company is described as India-focused). Net FX exposure is likely a modest **net negative** (short USD — buying PP in USD but selling films in INR). A 5% INR depreciation modestly compresses margins. Not a primary driver.

**Sector Cycle Verdict:** HEADWIND
**Primary driver:** Simultaneous capacity additions from multiple peers in FY26–FY27 into an already oversupplied domestic BOPP market.
**Expected duration:** 18–30 months of margin pressure before capacity is absorbed (FY27–FY28).
**DCF Impact:** Sector headwind → +5–10 percentage-point wider required MoS; do NOT embed FY26 margins as permanent.

## Module 3 — Business Quality & Moat

### Greenwald's Three Barriers

1. **Supply/Cost Advantage:** None identifiable. Nahar's two Bruckner lines are the same technology any competitor can buy. No captive PP sourcing, no scale advantage vs. Polyplex (7× larger), SRF, or Jindal. Gross margins are not sustainably above peers.

2. **Customer Captivity:** None. BOPP film for commodity applications is bought on price by converters who can switch between Nahar, Cosmo, and Polyplex within one purchase cycle. No switching costs, no contracts, no specification lock-in (for standard grades).

3. **Local Economies of Scale:** None. Mandideep, MP is not a region where Nahar has local dominance. It is one of many plants competing nationally.

**Moat Verdict: NONE.** This is a commodity manufacturer competing on cost efficiency and capacity utilisation.

### Buffett Moat Test
*Can Nahar Poly Films raise BOPP film prices by 10% without losing meaningful volume?*
**No.** Standard BOPP film prices are set by global supply-demand and domestic market conditions. A unilateral 10% price increase would immediately shift volume to Polyplex, Cosmo, or Jindal. Price takers, not price makers.

### Market Share Stability
Nahar's ~60,000 MTPA represents approximately 5–6% of India's ~1.1 million MTPA BOPP capacity. This share has been stable but reflects no competitive gain. The company is a small, regional player in a market dominated by Polyplex, SRF, Jindal, and Cosmo.

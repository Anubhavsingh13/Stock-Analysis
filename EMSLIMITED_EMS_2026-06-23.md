## BUSINESS PRIMER

EMS Limited is a New Delhi-headquartered multi-disciplinary EPC (Engineering, Procurement & Construction) company that specialises exclusively in water and wastewater infrastructure. Its core offering is turnkey project execution — from engineering design to construction and commissioning — for water treatment plants (WTPs), sewage treatment plants (STPs), common effluent treatment plants (CETPs), sewer networks, pumping stations, and water supply distribution schemes. Customers are almost entirely government bodies: state Jal Nigams, urban local bodies, municipal corporations, and central government agencies funded under Jal Jeevan Mission (JJM) and AMRUT 2.0. Revenue flows as project billing against milestones, supplemented by a smaller but growing Operations & Maintenance (O&M) stream. The water EPC segment accounts for ~95% of revenue; the O&M tail (5%) provides some annuity recurrence. The company depends on two things to earn its returns: (a) a sustained pipeline of government-sanctioned and -funded water projects, and (b) efficient on-site execution to convert order book into revenue and cash without excessive working capital consumption.

This is an asset-heavy, government-dependent EPC business operating in an inherently lumpy, project-based revenue model. The value driver is revenue velocity — how quickly the order book converts to invoices and then to cash — and the key risks are threefold: government payment timelines (the structural Achilles heel of all Indian infrastructure EPC companies), project execution disruptions from weather or site access, and the working capital trap that results when receivables balloon but creditors demand timely payment. EMS has two structural features that will colour this entire analysis: an extremely high working capital requirement (297 days as of Mar 2026 vs. typical EPC peers at 150–200 days), and a secular tailwind from India's single largest-ever water infrastructure programme — JJM 2.0 with a stated outlay of ₹8.69 lakh crore through December 2028. The central tension in this analysis is between a genuinely large and long-dated sector tailwind and a company that has demonstrably struggled to convert its order book into cash in FY26.

## MODULE 1b — Sector Cycle: Water Infrastructure EPC

### Secular vs. Cyclical Decomposition

The water EPC sector has a genuinely large secular driver: India's political commitment to universal piped water supply (JJM) and urban wastewater treatment (AMRUT 2.0, NGT mandates). Union Budget 2026-27 allocates ₹67,670 Cr to JJM alone — a 3.9× jump from the ₹17,000 Cr actually spent in FY25-26. Total water-related budget allocation is ₹2.16 lakh crore for FY27. JJM 2.0 extends through December 2028 with ₹8.69 lakh crore total programme.

**Critical insight:** FY26 saw a catastrophic execution gap — government budgeted ₹67,000 Cr for JJM but revised estimate came in at only ₹17,000 Cr (75% underspent). This is a sector-wide phenomenon, not EMS-specific. EMS's FY26 revenue decline from ₹966 Cr to ₹732 Cr (–24%) is a direct consequence.

**FY27 ramp-up credibility:** The ₹67,670 Cr JJM allocation for FY27 is identical to the number that went unspent in FY26. Structural execution constraints (state capacity, contractor mobilisation, land acquisition) do not vanish in one year. **Embed only 40–50% of the stated budget uplift as actually translating to EPC revenues in FY27.**

**Competitive capacity:** Water EPC is fragmented with 5–7 meaningful national players. No evidence of simultaneous large capacity builds or PLI-like flood of new entrants. Input cost environment neutral to slightly supportive (commodity prices softened in FY26). Net FX exposure: essentially zero (all projects domestic, INR-denominated).

**Sector Cycle Verdict:** Structural Tailwind (secular, 5–7 year duration) but near-term Cyclical Headwind (government underspend in FY26 creating an execution valley). Do NOT embed management guidance in full.

**DCF implication:** Mid-period growth rate = 18–20% (secular); do NOT embed 30%+ management CAGR target. EBITDA margin recovery to 22–24% (not 25–27% of FY20-22 given rising competition).

## MODULE 3 — Business Quality & Moat

### Greenwald's Three Barriers

1. **Supply/Cost Advantage:** Limited. EPC execution is learnable. EMS's advantage is pre-qualification credentials and project management expertise — not a structural cost edge. Gross margins not persistently above peers in manner that signals cost moat. **No moat.**

2. **Customer Captivity:** Near-zero. Government procurement is mandatorily competitive (L1 tendering). Every project is re-won. A Jal Nigam that gave EMS one contract can freely give the next to Va Tech Wabag. **No moat.**

3. **Local Economies of Scale:** Partial. EMS has geographic concentration in UP, Uttarakhand, and West Bengal — soft advantage in bidding accuracy. Not a durable moat preventing competition. **Weak, geographically bounded advantage only.**

**Buffett's Moat Test:** Could EMS raise prices 10% without losing contracts? Definitively NO — L1 bidding means lowest price wins every time.

### Fisher Point 15 (Integrity — Veto Criterion)

- Promoter pledge rose from 11% to 24–28% of 69.7% holding, secured against ₹210 Cr personal loan for personal real estate investment
- Repayment timeline already extended once (from FY26 commitment to FY27)
- FY26 guidance on PAT margin and EBITDA margin materially missed
- Investor concerns raised about "transparency and consistency in management communication"
- **NOT a Point 15 VETO** (no evidence of fraud/illegal activity), but a meaningful governance yellow flag

**Fisher 15-Point Score (estimated): ~55% — below the ~65% quality threshold**

**Moat Verdict:** No durable moat. The declining ROIC from 37% → 10–13% confirms the absence of a durable competitive advantage. EMS is a capable EPC executor in a government-funded market with weak structural defences.

## MODULE 6 — Earnings Quality & Financial Statements

**FCF vs. PAT (FY26): PAT ₹91 Cr, FCF –₹88 Cr. Earnings quality: POOR.**

The ₹179 Cr gap represents working capital consumption (receivables growth + WIP build). Pattern endemic to government EPC — revenue recognised on percentage-of-completion but cash comes 6–18 months later.

**Unbilled Revenue (₹283 Cr = 39% of FY26 revenue):** Work done but not yet invoiced. Real asset but subject to: (a) government approval of milestone claims, (b) quantity disputes, (c) mark-to-market risk if projects under-priced.

**Overdue Receivables (₹120 Cr >6 months):** Government clients >6 months overdue indicate financial difficulty, claim disputes, or stuck projects. At 16.4% of revenue — even 20% impairment = ₹24 Cr loss = 26% of FY26 PAT.

**Inventory Build (₹100 Cr WIP increase):** Materials deployed but revenue not yet booked — indicates projects in execution awaiting government permissions.

**Revenue Recognition Method:** Percentage-of-completion (POC) — IFRS-compliant but requires management judgment. In delay periods, companies can still book revenue based on work done even when invoicing lags significantly.

**Earnings Quality Verdict:** LOW. ~100% of FY26 PAT is accrual-based; FCF is negative. A 2–3 year time lag between earning and collecting is structural, but at 297 WC days, EMS's lag is materially worse than peers.

## 🔴 Executive Summary

Tembo Global Industries reports a spectacular growth record — revenue up 60% CAGR over five years to ₹1,090cr in FY26, PAT up 105% CAGR to ₹98cr, ROE 28%, all at a headline P/E of ~11× — and **almost none of it has ever turned into cash**. Over FY24–FY26 the company reported ₹163cr of cumulative net profit while generating **negative ₹150cr of cumulative operating cash flow**; the gap was funded by ₹336cr of new borrowings and ~₹230cr of fresh equity issued largely to non-promoter entities, during which promoter holding collapsed from 72.77% to 37.74% with 35.7% of the residual stake pledged. **The Beneish M-Score of −1.14 classifies this as a likely manipulator, the Piotroski F-Score is 3/9, the Sloan accrual ratio is 29%, and nine of fifteen O'Glove flags trigger.** Four independent hard stops fire. The underlying niche — UL/FM-approved pipe support systems — is a genuinely attractive global business dominated by Hilti and Walraven; **this listed vehicle is not a safe way to own it.** The low P/E is not a mispricing, it is the market correctly discounting earnings that do not exist in cash.

## BUSINESS PRIMER

Tembo Global Industries manufactures and trades fabricated metal products — pipe support and suspension systems, industrial fasteners, anchors, threaded rods, and HVAC components — sold business-to-business into oil and gas, marine, power, real estate, water, and infrastructure projects, and it increasingly bundles those products into full project execution as an EPC contractor across civil, MEP, and HVAC scopes. Revenue therefore arrives through three very different channels: volume × realisation on manufactured product, percentage-completion milestones on EPC contracts, and a bolted-on **trading business that includes textiles** — a legacy division entirely unrelated to engineering that grew 44.4% year-on-year in Q3FY26. Operations are centred on Vasai, Maharashtra, with an announced expansion from 15,000 MTPA to 100,000 MTPA, and the company holds Underwriters Laboratories and FM Approval (USA) certifications, which in this industry are genuine, hard-won entry barriers. The two things this business depends on to earn its returns are those certifications converting into specification lock-in at OEM and EPC customers, and its ability to collect from long-cycle infrastructure and Middle East project clients.

This is best classified as a **hybrid: a small genuine manufacturer wrapped inside a much larger working-capital-intensive EPC and trading operation**, and the three legs have irreconcilable economics that must never be blended. The key value driver management presents is volume and order book; the key risk is **cash conversion**, and it is not a peripheral risk but the central fact of the investment case. The structural feature that colours this entire analysis — and it is decisive — is that **83% of the balance sheet (₹1,089cr of ₹1,306cr total assets) sits in "other assets"**, i.e. receivables, inventory, and advances, against only ₹118cr of fixed assets producing ₹1,090cr of revenue. A company cannot manufacture ₹1,090cr of output from ₹118cr of plant; it can only trade or subcontract it. Every module below is interpreted through that lens.

## MODULE 1b — Sector Cycle: Fabricated Metal Products & Industrial EPC

### Secular vs. Cyclical Decomposition — **The Decisive Test**

FY26 revenue grew **46.7%** (₹744cr → ₹1,090cr). Management attributes this to infrastructure demand. Decomposing honestly:

| Component | Est. | Durability |
|---|---|---|
| **Secular** — India infra/oil & gas capex, port & terminal build-out, data-centre HVAC, Middle East offshore revamp | **~12–15pp** | Durable. This tailwind is **real**. |
| **Cyclical** — order-timing lumpiness, EPC milestone recognition | **~8–10pp** | Fades. |
| **Mix/acquisitive/trading expansion** — textile & engineering trading scale-up | **~20–25pp** | **Not economic growth at all.** Low-value-add topline. |

**Durable structural baseline: ~12–15%, not 47%.** Per the skill's explicit warning — *"A company reporting 35% growth that is 10% secular + 25% cyclical will mean-revert... Embedding 35% permanently inflates intrinsic value 3–4×."* Tembo's case is worse than the textbook example, because a large slice of its growth is **trading revenue that carries almost no economic value**.

### 🚩 The Margin Anomaly — The Single Most Implausible Fact in This File

| Year | Revenue | OPM% |
|---|---|---|
| FY22 | ₹176cr | **5%** |
| FY23 | ₹250cr | **5%** |
| FY24 | ₹439cr | **6%** |
| FY25 | ₹744cr | **12%** |
| FY26 | ₹1,090cr | **13%** |

Operating margin **more than doubled (6% → 13%) precisely as the revenue mix shifted toward EPC and trading.** This is backwards. EPC carries 8–14% EBITDA and trading carries 2–5% — both structurally **below** the 15–22% of the manufacturing base. Growing the low-margin legs from ~40% to ~65% of revenue should have **compressed** blended margin toward 8–10%. Instead it doubled.

There are only three explanations: (a) the manufacturing leg's margin expanded so violently it offset the mix drag — implausible in a steel-input, competitive niche; (b) the EPC/trading margins being booked are not real EPC/trading margins — i.e. transfer or related-party pricing; (c) **costs are being capitalised into the ₹1,089cr "other assets" line rather than expensed.** Given the negative operating cash flow, **(c) is the reading most consistent with the evidence.** Management has not addressed this in any disclosure I could locate.

### Other Sector Inputs
- **Input cost:** Steel — volatile, partial pass-through. Should pressure margin. It has not. See above.
- **Competitive capacity pipeline:** Tembo is adding 85,000 MTPA (15k → 100k). No evidence of coordinated peer additions, but a 6.7× capacity expansion into a niche is itself an oversupply risk **it creates**.
- **Geopolitical:** Middle East EPC exposure (Kuwait offshore revamp, ~₹300cr L1). **Classification: STRUCTURAL** — Gulf government-linked receivables are slow and politically contingent, not re-routable in a quarter. → **widen MoS 5–10pp.**
- **Net FX exposure:** Middle East EPC revenue in USD/KWD against largely INR costs → net long foreign currency. Not quantified in disclosures. **Gap.**

**Sector cycle verdict: TAILWIND (genuine, multi-year, structural).**
**This is important and I want to be fair about it: the industry is not the problem.** India's infra capex and Gulf offshore revamp spending are real, durable demand. The problem is entirely company-specific.

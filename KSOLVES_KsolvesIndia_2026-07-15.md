## Executive Summary
Ksolves is a capital-light, founder-run IT-services microcap (Salesforce, Odoo ERP, Big Data, AI/ML; 89% export revenue) that compounded revenue ~58% CAGR at freakishly high ~43% operating margins. Those anomalous margins have mean-reverted — but, on verification, the reversion has **largely played out and stabilised near ~30% EBITDA** (FY26 29.7%, Q4 FY26 rising sequentially to 29.3%), exactly where management guides medium-term. FY26 EPS still printed down YoY (₹14.44→₹14.66→₹13.95) only because FY25 *began* at higher-margin quarters; with margins now flat ~30% and revenue guided +18–20%, **EPS growth should resume in FY27** (est. ~₹16–17 → forward P/E ~19x, PEG ~1.0). Cash conversion is healthy (OCF/PAT ~0.94–0.97), there is no pledge, the brief FY25 borrowing was a benign dividend-bridge since repaid, and management's guidance track record is credible (WTT ~0.83). It is a genuinely high-quality capital-light cash machine (~4–5% yield, negligible capital needs) trading at a *fair-to-full* trailing multiple that looks more reasonable on forward earnings. The residual risks are microcap client concentration, GenAI deflating billable hours, and margins drifting toward the 25% floor rather than holding 30%.

## Business Primer

Ksolves India sells outsourced software engineering and IT consulting to enterprises, overwhelmingly export (≈89% of revenue, largely USD), from delivery centres in Noida, Pune and Indore with ~600–700 engineers. It makes money the classic services way — billing people-hours and fixed-price projects — but concentrated in a few high-value niches where it holds top-tier vendor certifications: Salesforce (Summit/Platinum partner), Odoo ERP (Gold/Best-Partner-India), Adobe/Magento commerce, Apache Big Data (Databricks partner), and now an "AI-first" push around agentic/ML services. Its growth engine is a "land-and-expand" model on Fortune-500-type accounts. The two things the business absolutely depends on are (1) its ability to keep winning and expanding a small number of large offshore accounts, and (2) keeping billing rates well above fully-loaded engineer cost — the spread that produced its abnormal margins.

This is a **capital-light services compounder**, not an asset play — it needs almost no fixed capital (net block ~₹6cr on ₹161cr revenue), throws off cash, and pays nearly all of it out as dividends, which is why its ROE/ROCE optically read 130%+ (a tiny equity base, not a magical business). The dominant value driver is **operating margin**, and the dominant risk is **margin mean-reversion**: a 43% OPM is unheard-of for IT services (TCS/Infosys ~24–26%, mid-caps ~18–22%), and it was almost certainly a small-scale, under-invested-in-overhead artefact. The whole analysis must be read through this lens — the reported "127% ROCE / 58% CAGR" history describes a phase that is visibly ending, and the investable question is the *normalised* margin and growth on the other side of that reset, not the trailing glory numbers. A secondary structural feature to watch is USD revenue concentration (FX-sensitive) and heavy customer concentration on a small revenue base.

## Module 6 — Earnings Quality & ROIC / DuPont

- **ROIC / ROCE: 127%, ROE: 132%** — *optically extreme, and misleading.* These are artefacts of a near-zero equity/capital base (≈100% payout keeps book value tiny at ₹12.2/share). The correct read: **the business needs almost no incremental capital to grow → every rupee of profit is (in principle) distributable.** That is genuinely valuable *if* profit grows; it is a trap if you mistake the ratio for pricing power.
- **The real earnings-quality story is margin, not fraud:** OPM 43%→29%, PAT margin ~26%→~21%. Absolute PAT flat-to-down (₹34→₹35→₹33cr) despite revenue +49% over two years. **Operating leverage is running in reverse** — costs (people, sales/events) scaling faster than revenue.
- **DuPont read:** ROE stayed high only because the tiny equity base and payout structure hold the denominator down; the *operating* engine (EBIT margin × asset turnover) is **deteriorating on the margin leg**. Asset turnover remains very high (capital-light) — that is stable/structural. So the ROE decline is **purely operational (margin), not financial engineering** — which is the "honest" kind of deterioration, but deterioration nonetheless.

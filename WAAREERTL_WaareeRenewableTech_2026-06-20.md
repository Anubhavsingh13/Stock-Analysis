## BUSINESS PRIMER

### What the business does
Waaree Renewable Technologies Limited (WRTL) is the solar EPC (Engineering, Procurement & Construction) arm of the Waaree Group. It designs, engineers, procures, and commissions ground-mounted and rooftop solar power plants for third-party customers — utility-scale IPPs, PSUs, industrial captive users, and government agencies. It does not primarily own or operate solar power plants (though it has a nascent IPP pipeline of 65.5 MWp approved capacity). Revenue flows through construction milestone billing under Ind AS 115 (Percentage of Completion Method), making it lumpy quarter-to-quarter. In FY26, WRTL doubled both revenue (₹3,331 Cr, +108.5% YoY) and profit (PAT ₹478.65 Cr, +109% YoY), driven by India's accelerating solar installation pace. The unexecuted order book of 2.83 GWp (estimated value ₹11,000–13,000 Cr at ₹4–4.5 Cr/MWp) provides 2–3 years of revenue visibility at current execution pace. The bidding pipeline of 27–36 GWp is approximately 10× the current order book — the limiting factor is not market demand, it is the company's own project execution capacity. Two adjacent opportunities are being pursued: Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS) EPC and data centre infrastructure — both at early stage.

### What kind of business this is
WRTL is an asset-light, project-execution business. Its capital base is thin (equity ~₹694 Cr on ₹3,331 Cr revenue = AT of 4.8×) because it deploys human capital and relationship capital rather than physical assets. This produces exceptional return metrics — ROE 68.93%, ROCE 62.54% — that are among the highest in the Indian listed mid-cap universe. The business monetises three advantages: (1) the Waaree Group brand and track record (critical for winning large government/PSU tenders); (2) potential cost advantage in module procurement from group company Waaree Energies; and (3) execution speed and quality (which drives repeat business). The key analytical question is margin sustainability: at 19.2% EBITDA margin, WRTL earns roughly 60–140% MORE than comparable EPC peers (8–12% range). Whether this premium reflects structural advantage (group module sourcing, execution excellence) or temporary conditions (project mix, early-stage scale) is the central investment thesis debate. Given the execution-constrained (not demand-constrained) growth profile, the business is fundamentally higher quality than a typical EPC contractor.

## MODULE 1b — Sector Cycle

**Assessment: VERY STRONG STRUCTURAL TAILWIND**

**Secular demand for solar EPC:**
India is adding 20–25 GW of solar per year and needs to accelerate to 35–40 GW/year to meet the 500 GW RE target by 2030. Every GW of solar requires EPC services — RPO mandates for DISCOMs, PM Surya Ghar (10M rooftop homes), SECI utility tenders, and industrial decarbonisation are all structural demand drivers. Unlike module manufacturing (which can be done offshore and imported), EPC is a domestic services business — it cannot be outsourced to China. This is pure secular, policy-mandated demand in India.

**BESS adjacency:** India's grid requires battery storage as solar penetration rises (evening peak demand vs afternoon solar surplus). SECI is bundling BESS with solar in increasingly large tenders. BESS EPC is higher-margin and more technically complex than pure solar EPC — ideal for an operator building technical differentiation. WRTL has begun bidding on BESS projects; this is a significant adjacency that could double the addressable market by FY29.

**Data centre adjacency:** India data centre capacity is doubling as hyperscalers enter (Google, Microsoft, Amazon) and domestic AI/cloud demand grows. Data centres need reliable, industrial-grade power infrastructure — WRTL's capability in electrical systems positions it as a potential EPC contractor for this segment.

**Competition intensity note:** L&T, Adani, Sterling Wilson Renewable, KPI Green Energy, and multiple regional players compete aggressively. Government L1 bidding (lowest price wins) keeps margins under pressure for commodity EPC work. WRTL's premium margin suggests it is winning on a basis other than lowest price — likely execution quality, balance sheet strength, or intra-group module advantage.

**Sector verdict: VERY STRONG TAILWIND. Domestic policy-mandated demand; EPC cannot be offshored; BESS adjacency doubles the TAM.**

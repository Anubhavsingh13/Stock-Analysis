## Executive Summary
Gujjubhai Industries is a ₹255–425 cr micro-cap (share count ambiguous post-merger) that reached the public market via a **related-party reverse merger**: the promoter's own private company, Gujjubhai Foods Pvt Ltd, was amalgamated into the listed shell Sumuka Agro Industries in Feb–Mar 2026 (promoter Paresh Thakker sat on both sides). ## BUSINESS PRIMER

**What it does / how it makes money.** The company sells packaged and traded food products in India — dry fruits, namkeen and traditional snacks, khakhra/bhakhri, instant bhel, Himalayan pink salt, mineral water, ready-to-cook items, sweets and spices — under the "GujjuBhai Foods" brand, through trading, retailing, franchising and online channels. Revenue is overwhelmingly **volume × realization on traded/packaged food**, and the economics read like distribution/trading, not branded FMCG: operating margin is ~6% (a genuine branded-FMCG gross margin would be 30–50% with 12–20% EBITDA). Revenue exploded from **₹1.39 cr (FY22) → ₹54.95 cr (FY24) → ₹127 cr (FY26)** — a trajectory driven not by organic brand-building but by **acquiring trading companies and merging them in via share swaps**. The most recent example (Arkaa Cluster, a May-2022-incorporated agro-commodity *trader* doing ₹150 cr turnover, bought for just ₹35 cr in paper while simultaneously being lent up to ₹35 cr) makes the model explicit: buy revenue with the listed shell's stock, dress it as an FMCG brand.

**What kind of business / what to watch.** Classify this as a **low-quality, promoter-controlled micro-cap trading roll-up wrapped in an FMCG narrative**, not a capital-light consumer compounder. The dominant value "driver" is acquired revenue, the most fragile kind; the dominant risk is **governance and capital leakage** — a related-party reverse merger, serial share issuance, interest-free loans to acquired/related entities, and loans to *struck-off companies* flagged by the auditor. The single structural feature colouring the entire analysis: this is a **backdoor-listed shell that changed its name and business identity in the same window it was issuing large blocks of stock to the promoter** — the textbook setting for value transfer away from minority holders. Every reported number must be treated as guilty until proven cash-backed.

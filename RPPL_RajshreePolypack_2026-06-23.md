## Executive Summary

Rajshree Polypack is a micro-cap rigid plastic packaging manufacturer with blue-chip customers (Amul, Ferrero, Britannia, Tata) in a structurally growing Indian food & beverage packaging market. These are genuine positives. However, the stock fails on four decisive dimensions: ROIC has been persistently below WACC for the entire decade (value-destroying growth); promoter holding has declined 8.5pp in 3 years with no institutional absorption (bearish insider signal); the Cash Conversion Cycle of 185 days is 2–3× the peer benchmark and structurally constrains FCF; and at ₹20.6, margin of safety is absent — DCF base case intrinsic value is ₹11–22, SVAR is 78%, upside/downside ratio is sub-1.5:1. A value trap at current price; investable only below ₹11–13 if governance resolves.

## Business Primer

Rajshree Polypack manufactures two interconnected categories of rigid plastic packaging: extruded base sheets (PP, PET, APET, GPET, HIPS and high-barrier variants) that other packagers use for form-fill-seal (FFS) machines, and thermoformed finished food packaging containers (yogurt/ice cream cups, beverage containers, bakery trays, retort-ready pouches, injection-moulded portion cups) sold directly to food and beverage companies. Revenue is fundamentally volume × realization: the company processes polymer pellets through extrusion lines (~25,000 MT/year combined capacity) and thermoforming/injection moulding machines (~5,800 MTPA finished capacity) to produce output sold to brand owners including Amul, Ferrero, Britannia, Tata Global Beverages, Unilever, Epigamia, Domino's, and Swiggy. All three manufacturing plants are in Gujarat (Sarigam, Valsad) and Daman (Kachigam and Somnath-Dabhel units). The business depends absolutely on two things: competitive procurement of polymer pellets (PP, PET, HIPS from petrochemical suppliers, priced globally), and sustained volume growth in India's organised food and beverage sector.

This is an asset-heavy, capital-intensive manufacturing business with commodity-adjacent economics — it operates upstream of the branded food and beverage value chain, selling enabling packaging rather than a branded product. The primary value driver is volume growth (throughput in MT) combined with mix improvement toward value-added products like barrier packaging and retort applications, which command higher realization per MT. The structurally important features that colour this entire analysis are three: first, working capital intensity is extreme, with a cash conversion cycle of ~185 days as of FY26, meaning the company carries nearly six months of working capital funded by debt (borrowings rose from ₹8 Cr in FY20 to ₹123 Cr in FY26, a 15× increase in six years); second, promoter holding has declined ~8.5 percentage points over the past three years (52.5% → 44.0%), which warrants investigation; and third, ROCE has remained structurally below what a small-cap manufacturer's WACC should be (~13–15%), raising the fundamental question of whether recent capex has been value-creating or value-destroying.

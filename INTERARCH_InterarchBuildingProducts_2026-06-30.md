## Executive Summary

Interarch Building Solutions is India's second-largest Pre-Engineered Building (PEB) manufacturer — asset-light, debt-free, and riding a genuine multi-year structural tailwind from private sector industrial capex, data centres, and PLI-linked manufacturing. The business has compounded revenue at 27% CAGR over FY21–FY26 with ROIC climbing from single digits to ~23%, and management has credibly guided to ₹2,200 crore revenue in FY27 and ₹2,500 crore in FY28. However, at ₹1,913/share the stock is trading at ~24× FY26 earnings and ~18–20× EV/EBITDA — a valuation that already prices in the optimistic growth trajectory with thin margin of safety. The key risk is the short operating history as a listed entity (IPO August 2024), a margin structure tethered to volatile HR coil steel prices (~65% of revenue), and significant new capacity coming online in FY27 from both Interarch and competitor Kirby Building Systems simultaneously, which could pressure realisation.

## Business Primer

**Paragraph 1 — What Interarch does and how it makes money:**
Interarch Building Solutions Ltd (formerly Interarch Building Products) is a project-based manufacturer and erector of Pre-Engineered Steel Buildings (PEB) — factory-fabricated structural steel frames, wall and roof cladding panels, and related ancillaries supplied as complete, ready-to-assemble building kits. The company designs, fabricates, delivers, and installs these systems predominantly for industrial and logistics customers: Amazon warehouses, Delhivery distribution centres, Mahindra Logistics facilities, DHL hubs, and increasingly, data centres, cold storage facilities, and semiconductor/EV battery plants being built under India's PLI programmes. Revenue flows through a project model — customer wins an order (₹1.5 crore to ₹150 crore per contract, average ~₹12 crore), Interarch fabricates at one of its plants (Uttarakhand, Andhra Pradesh, and new Gujarat facility), and revenue is recognised on percentage-of-completion basis over a 4–12 month execution cycle. The business absolutely depends on two things to earn its returns: (1) a steady inflow of new industrial capex orders from corporate India, and (2) the spread between realisation locked in fixed-price contracts and the actual HR coil steel procurement cost over the execution period.

**Paragraph 2 — What kind of business this is and what to watch:**
Interarch is an asset-light, asset-heavy hybrid: it carries a high gross asset turnover of ~8–10× (each ₹50–60 crore plant can generate ₹450–500 crore of revenue), yet its fortunes are governed entirely by the volume and pace of private sector capital expenditure — making it cyclical rather than a steady compounder. The key value driver is volume × order-mix: more orders of higher complexity (data centres, multi-storey structures) at stable steel prices generate disproportionate margin improvement. The key risk is the steel price cycle — HR coil constitutes ~65–68% of revenue and moves independently of Interarch's realisation on fixed-price contracts signed 6–12 months prior, creating margin vulnerability in rising-steel-price periods. The structural feature that will colour the entire analysis is Interarch's position at the intersection of two powerful but potentially temporary tailwinds: the current private capex upcycle and the one-time burst from PLI-linked factory construction — both of which could moderate post-FY27, leaving a more normalised 12–15% organic growth rate. The company has been listed only since August 2024 (IPO), so governance track record as a public entity is limited; however, the two promoter families (Nandas and Suris) have ~30 years of operating history and zero promoter pledge.

## Module 1b — Sector Cycle: Tailwinds & Headwinds

*Source: IMARC Group PEB market reports, company concalls, DRHP*

### Secular vs. Cyclical Demand Decomposition

**Observed revenue growth FY21–FY26:** ~27% revenue CAGR
- **Secular component (durable):** ~12–15% — driven by (a) India PEB market structural growth of 12–15% CAGR replacing RCC construction in industrial segment, (b) logistics sector formalisation post-GST, (c) e-commerce warehousing buildout, (d) data centre construction surge (secular for 5–7 years), (e) PLI-linked factory construction
- **Cyclical component (temporary):** ~10–12% — driven by (a) post-COVID industrial recovery and deferred capex catch-up FY22–24, (b) government infrastructure capex peak FY23–24 (some spill-over into PEB via airports, metro, defence), (c) one-time PLI-scheme front-loading of factory investments FY23–25

**Critical decomposition verdict:** Of FY26's 30.6% growth, approximately 15–18% is structural/recurring and 12–15% is cyclical — either one-time PLI front-loading now largely executed, or government-capex-linked orders that will moderate in FY27–28. This is the single most important analytical point: embedding 30% growth into a forward DCF would be a serious error.

**Durable baseline for DCF mid-period growth:** 15–18% (vs. management's aspirational 20%+)

### Input Cost Cycle
HR coil steel prices averaged ₹53,000–58,000/tonne in FY26, down from ~₹62,000+ peak in FY22. Currently in a relatively benign steel price environment. China's overcapacity and weak domestic demand have depressed global steel prices — modestly positive for Interarch's margins on fixed-price contracts. Risk: US Section 232 tariffs divert Chinese steel to Southeast Asia → possible dumping in India → Indian mills MSRP pressure. Stable-to-slightly-declining steel = margin-supportive.

### Competitive Capacity Pipeline
Kirby Building Systems commissioned its new Gujarat facility in FY26 (50,000 MTPA starting capacity, expandable to 100,000 MTPA). Interarch is simultaneously adding ~40,000 MTPA (Gujarat 20,000 + AP Phase 1 20,000) in H1FY27. Pennar Industries has its own expansion plans. **Simultaneous capacity additions = potential absorption risk in FY27–28 if demand growth pauses.** This is a moderate-to-serious sector headwind risk.

### Net FX Exposure
Export revenue: ~₹40 crore in FY26 (~2.1% of revenue). Material USD/INR exposure is negligible at current scale. Export target ₹100 crore in FY27 (still <5% of revenue) — not a meaningful FX variable. Treat as neutral.

**Sector Cycle Verdict: Tailwind (Moderating)**
**Primary driver:** Private sector industrial capex + data centre construction
**Expected duration:** Secular tailwind continues; cyclical component (PLI front-loading) moderating in FY27
**DCF impact:** Use 15–17% mid-period growth (not management's 20% guidance) for base case

## Module 2b — Business Quality & Moat

*Reference: references/01-moat-analysis.md*

### Greenwald's Three Barriers

1. **Supply/Cost Advantage:** Partial. Interarch's manufacturing scale (second-largest in India), long-standing supplier relationships with Tata Steel/SAIL, and 40+ years of accumulated engineering know-how create mild cost advantages. Gross margins are not materially above peers (8–11% range shared with Pennar). Not a strong cost moat.

2. **Customer Captivity:** Moderate. The 81% repeat order rate (FY24) is the strongest evidence of customer captivity — once a company trusts a PEB manufacturer for one facility, the switching cost for repeat orders is high (pre-qualification records, proven track record, existing design relationships). However, large corporates like Amazon or DHL can and do multi-source, limiting pricing power. **Buffett moat test:** Could Interarch raise prices 10% without losing meaningful volume? Unlikely in the short-run for commodity warehousing; possible for complex structures like data centres. **PARTIAL PASS.**

3. **Local Economies of Scale:** Weak nationally; stronger regionally. The Uttarakhand plant serves North India efficiently; AP plant serves South India; Gujarat plant will target West India/EV corridor. Transport economics (PEB structures are bulky; freight costs meaningful) create mild geographic moats around each plant. Not dominant enough to constitute a strong structural barrier.

**Moat Verdict: Narrow**
The moat is primarily customer captivity (81% repeat rate + pre-qualification barriers) and mild regional scale. It is real but not wide — it protects margins in normal conditions but would not prevent market share erosion if a better-capitalised competitor (Kirby, Tata BlueScope) pursues aggressive pricing.

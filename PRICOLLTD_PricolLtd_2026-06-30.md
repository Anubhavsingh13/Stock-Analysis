## Executive Summary

Pricol Limited is India's second-largest instrument cluster manufacturer globally by volume, with ~55–60% domestic market share and a commanding 65% position in the two-wheeler segment. The company is undergoing a transformative demerger announced on June 27, 2026, splitting into Pricol Autotech Limited (housing the DICVS tech-led business — ₹2,425 Cr revenue) and a retained Pricol Limited (ACFMS + Precision Products — ₹1,628 Cr revenue). The demerger represents a compelling SOTP opportunity where the combined entity is likely worth significantly more as two focused listed companies than as one blended holding. The key risk is a 12–18 month NCLT timeline creating carry cost, combined with valuation that at the parent level is already pricing in substantial value unlock at 28× trailing P/E.

## Business Primer

Pricol Limited manufactures and supplies instrument clusters, oil pumps, water pumps, sensors, telematics units, disc brakes, and precision engineering components primarily to the Indian two-wheeler, three-wheeler, passenger vehicle, and commercial vehicle OEM ecosystem. Revenue flows through two principal verticals: the Driver Information and Connected Vehicle Solutions (DICVS) segment — comprising analog, digital, LCD, and TFT instrument clusters, e-cockpits, telematics, and battery management systems — which generates approximately 61% of consolidated revenue (₹2,425 Cr in FY26); and the Actuation, Control and Fluid Management Systems (ACFMS) segment — comprising fuel pumps, oil pumps, water pumps, disc brakes, and sensors — which contributes roughly 20–22% of revenue with a growing precision products subsidiary (P3L) handling the balance. Major customers are Bajaj Auto, TVS Motor Company, Hero MotoCorp, Tata Motors (75–80% of Tata's cars now use Pricol clusters), Honda, Ashok Leyland, and off-highway OEMs in export markets. The two things Pricol absolutely depends on for returns are: (a) Indian two-wheeler and passenger vehicle volume growth, and (b) content-per-vehicle expansion driven by electronics premiumisation.

This is a capital-light-within-manufacturing business — reasonably asset-light for instrument clusters (high ROCE of 25.9% in FY26) but capex-heavy in its current growth phase (₹680–700 Cr guided for FY27). The key value driver is content-per-vehicle growth rather than pure OEM volume: as clusters migrate from analog to digital to TFT and add connectivity, Pricol captures a rising share of vehicle bill-of-materials without necessarily requiring proportional OEM volume growth. The key risks are technology disruption (the EV transition demands new electronic architectures — an opportunity Pricol is actively addressing but not yet proven in), customer concentration (top 3–4 OEMs likely represent 70%+ of revenue), and the 12–18 month demerger execution risk which hangs over the stock during the NCLT process. The structural feature that will colour this entire analysis is the June 2026 demerger announcement, which fundamentally changes the valuation framework — Pricol should now be analysed as two businesses, not one.

## MODULE 3 — Business Quality & Moat

### Greenwald's Three Barriers

**1. Cost / Supply Advantage:** PARTIAL. Pricol does not have a unique raw material or manufacturing cost advantage. However, at 30 million clusters/year capacity with 55–60% domestic share, it benefits from scale-driven procurement advantages (semiconductor volume discounts, tooling amortization). Scale advantage would be difficult for a new entrant to replicate without 3–5 years of OEM qualification and significant investment. **Verdict: Narrow cost advantage through scale**

**2. Customer Captivity:** STRONG. Instrument clusters are deeply embedded in OEM design cycles. Each model year, a new cluster requires 12–18 months of co-development with the OEM, qualification testing, homologation, and tooling amortization. Once a supplier wins a model program, they supply for the entire model life (3–7 years) with no meaningful risk of mid-cycle switching. Management disclosed Letters of Intent ensuring "stable market share for three years" — confirming OEM captivity. Additionally, Pricol's co-development capability in TFT and e-cockpit creates relationship stickiness beyond mere supply agreements. **Verdict: Genuine switching cost moat**

**3. Local Economies of Scale:** STRONG in 2W segment. 65% domestic 2W cluster share creates a scale flywheel — the more models Pricol qualifies, the more OEMs want Pricol because of its validation experience and faster time-to-market for new variants. A new entrant cannot cost-effectively serve 65% of the market from scratch. **Verdict: Local scale monopoly in 2W clusters**

**Buffett Moat Test:** Can Pricol raise prices 10%? Partially — cluster pricing is OEM-negotiated annually, so pricing power is constrained in the short term. However, the migration to higher-value TFT clusters is effectively a 2–4× realization increase vs analog clusters — a form of value-driven pricing power disguised as product mix shift. **Partial Pass.**

**Moat Verdict: NARROW-TO-WIDE** — Genuine customer captivity and local scale in 2W; constrained pricing power due to OEM buyer concentration.

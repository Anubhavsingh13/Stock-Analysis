## Business Primer

Suyog Telematics Limited is a passive telecom infrastructure company — a "towerco" — registered as Infrastructure Provider Category-I (IP-1) with the Department of Telecommunications, founded in 1995 and listed on BSE (537259) and NSE (SUYOG). The company owns, builds, and leases telecom towers, rooftop sites, high-power small cells, and optical fibre cable (OFC) networks to major operators: Jio, Airtel, Vodafone Idea, and BSNL account for over 95% of revenue. Revenue flows from long-term lease agreements (7–10 year contracts) at a fixed monthly rental per tenancy with annual escalations of approximately 2.5% and exit penalties — giving the business a highly recurring, contracted cashflow character with near-zero variable cost per additional tenant. As of Q4 FY26, the company operates 6,008 towers and 7,318 tenancies across 26 states and union territories. The business depends on two things to earn its returns: owning tower assets in locations operators cannot easily replicate (rooftops, flyovers, sea-links, government sites) and maintaining long-term anchor tenancies that generate near-fixed revenue streams. Revenue per tower is approximately ₹2,900–₹3,500 per month for private operator towers; BSNL sites earn significantly less, resulting in a blended average of ~₹2,900/month.

Suyog is an asset-heavy infrastructure / landlord business — structurally similar to a REIT in cashflow character but capital-intensive in its growth phase. The key value driver is tenancy ratio: each incremental tenant loaded on an existing tower costs near-zero marginal cash but generates ₹20,000–₹35,000 per month in near-pure EBITDA, making this a structurally high-operating-leverage model. The current tenancy ratio of 1.22x is meaningfully below the sector benchmark (Indus Towers ~1.7x), representing both a competitive weakness and the primary upside lever — every 0.1x improvement in tenancy ratio at current scale adds approximately ₹18–22 Cr in EBITDA annually. Key risks are customer concentration (>95% revenue from 4 operators), execution risk (FY26 revenue guidance of ₹240 Cr was missed by 13%), the funding gap in a ₹500 Cr capex plan (₹200 Cr still to be arranged), and rising interest costs. One structural feature colours the entire analysis: the apparent FY25 PAT collapse from ₹63 Cr to ₹41 Cr was almost entirely attributable to a ₹27.5 Cr non-cash ESOP charge; the underlying operating business was improving, confirmed by FY26 EBITDA margin recovery to 74%.

## Executive Summary
IKIO Technologies is a contract manufacturer (ODM/EMS) of LED lighting products that IPO'd in June 2023, historically earning ~90%+ of its revenue-model economics from supplying a small number of brands (dominated by Signify/Philips) rather than selling under its own name. Since listing, revenue has kept growing (₹438cr→₹595cr, FY24→FY26) but EBITDA margin has nearly halved (21%→12-13%) and ROCE has collapsed (22%→9%, H1FY26 5.6%), while a ~₹263cr new-facility capex program has coincided with **falling**, not rising, EBIT — a quantifiable capital-allocation red flag. The stock is down ~50% from its post-listing highs and hit an all-time low in March 2026, yet still trades at a rich 40x trailing P/E and 2.5x book because earnings collapsed faster than the price. Combined with a structurally weak moat (no long-term customer contracts, extreme buyer power), declining institutional ownership, and a technical downtrend, the risk/reward is unfavorable at the current price.

## Business Primer

IKIO Technologies (formerly IKIO Lighting Limited, renamed March 2025) is an Original Design Manufacturer (ODM) and Electronics Manufacturing Services (EMS) company that designs and manufactures LED lighting products — over 600 SKUs spanning LED lights, LED drivers, refrigeration lighting and controls, switches and hardware, and recreational-vehicle (RV) components — which it supplies to brand-owner customers who then sell the products under their own names. Its single largest customer historically has been Signify Innovations India Ltd (formerly Philips), which has accounted for as much as ~90%+ of revenue in early fiscal periods and roughly ~50% more recently, with the top 20 customers together contributing 85–91% of revenue; other named customers include Honeywell, Frigoglass, Panasonic Life Solutions and Western Refrigeration. Critically, IKIO has no long-term supply contracts with any customer — deal validity typically runs only 2–3 years — so revenue flows from repeat commercial wins rather than contracted backlog. Manufacturing is concentrated in four in-house facilities (Haridwar, Uttarakhand, plus three in Noida/NCR) with combined stated capacity of "over 20 million pieces" annually; a fifth, larger facility on the Noida Expressway (~5 lakh sq. ft., ~₹263 crore project cost, funded substantially from IPO proceeds) is being commissioned in two blocks, with Block I live since May 2024 and Block II expected around Q1 FY27. International sales, routed partly through US subsidiary Royalux LLC, were roughly 18–22% of revenue in FY24–FY25 and are the company's stated diversification lever away from Signify-India dependency and toward US "China+1" tailwinds.

This is best classified as a capital-intensive contract manufacturer, not a branded consumer or platform business — its economics run on volume × unit realization within customer-set pricing, not on pricing power of its own. The single most important structural fact colouring every module below is customer concentration paired with the absence of long-term contracts: a business this dependent on one buyer's goodwill, renewed only every 2–3 years, has structurally weak bargaining power, and the post-listing margin compression (EBITDA margin ~21–23% pre-IPO/FY20–23 down to ~12–13% FY25–26) is consistent with that buyer power being exercised. A second structural feature to watch throughout: the company is mid-capex-cycle (a new plant nearly doubling its Noida footprint) funded partly by the 2023 IPO proceeds, and the central analytical question this report answers is whether that capex is currently earning adequate returns — the data suggests, as of FY26, that it is not yet.

## Module 1b — Sector Cycle: Tailwinds & Headwinds

**Secular vs. cyclical decomposition:** India's LED lighting market is estimated (sources diverge materially: $4.9bn–$10bn depending on research firm) growing at 6.5–9% CAGR — a moderate secular replacement/penetration story (incandescent/CFL→LED conversion largely complete in urban India; growth now tracks construction activity, exports, and product mix upgrades). IKIO's own revenue growth (FY24→FY26: 438→595cr, ~16.6% CAGR) has run faster than this secular market rate, which means either (a) genuine share gains, or (b) a cyclical boost from the Noida capacity ramp and new customer wins that may not repeat. Given the extreme buyer concentration, at least part of the recent growth is customer-specific (win/loss risk) rather than market-wide secular growth — this should **not** be extrapolated at 16%+ into a DCF.

**Input cost cycle:** LED components (chips, driver ICs) are import-exposed (largely China-sourced industry-wide); rupee depreciation and any China-specific tariff/trade friction are margin risks not fully diversified away.

**Geopolitical/FX:** Export mix (~18-22%, mostly the Royalux/US channel) exposes IKIO to US tariff policy — one source references Royalux "facing challenges" meeting a stated ~$8mn revenue target, attributed partly to "US market conditions, including tariff impacts" (unverified against primary transcript). This cuts both ways: China+1 sourcing shifts are a structural tailwind for Indian LED exporters generally, but US tariffs on the finished product category are a live risk specific to the export channel IKIO is trying to scale.

**Sector cycle verdict: Neutral-to-mild headwind.** Secular growth is moderate (high single digits), not the double-digit rate IKIO needs to justify its recent growth premium; export channel carries transient tariff-policy risk.

## Module 8 — Valuation: PIE First, Then Own Model

### 8A. Price-Implied Expectations (Reverse DCF, approximate)
Using FY26 NOPAT ≈ ₹48cr (EBIT ₹61cr × (1 − 20.8% effective tax rate)), an approximate WACC of ~14% (Rf 7% + β~1.2 × ERP 6%, blended with a small debt weight), and an estimated EV of ~₹1,500cr (market cap ₹1,522cr + debt ₹70cr, cash netting not resolvable from available data — flagged as an approximation):

```
PIE_implied_g ≈ (EV × WACC − NOPAT) / (EV + NOPAT)
             ≈ (1,500 × 14% − 48) / (1,500 + 48)
             ≈ (210 − 48) / 1,548 ≈ 10–12%
```

**The market is pricing roughly 10-12% perpetual NOPAT growth into IKIO at the current price.** Given that EBIT actually *fell* over the last two years (see Module 1c) and the business has no established moat, this is an **optimistic gap** — the price already assumes a durable growth/margin recovery that the company has not yet demonstrated.

Current price (₹197) is roughly **90% above** the Graham Number — fails the conservative screen decisively. Graham's combined multiplier test (P/E × P/B ≤ 22.5) also fails badly: 40.3 × 2.54 ≈ **102**, more than 4× the threshold.

```
Upside to Bull case:  (240 − 197) / 197 ≈ +22%

Upside/Downside Ratio ≈ 0.29 : 1  →  far below the 2:1 minimum required
```

**Margin of Safety: Inadequate — negative, in fact, since even the probability-weighted expected value sits well below the current price.** By the "why is it mispriced" lens: the stock's ~50% fall from post-listing highs may be creating an appearance of cheapness (a classic behavioral trap), but the *earnings* have fallen faster than the price, leaving the P/E and P/B multiples still elevated relative to what the current operating trajectory supports.

## Module 10 — Forward View: Catalysts, Risks, Scenarios

### Step 6 — Incremental ROIC Check (the key finding of this report)
```
Growth capex (FY24–FY26, from net block + CWIP delta) ≈ ₹208cr
Incremental EBIT (FY24 → FY26) = ₹61cr − ₹97cr = −₹36cr  (DECLINE, not increase)
Incremental ROIC ≈ NEGATIVE
WACC ≈ 14%
```
**Verdict: Value-destroying, as measured through FY26.** The company has spent roughly a quarter of its own market cap on a new manufacturing facility over the past two years, and EBIT is *lower*, not higher, than before that spending began.

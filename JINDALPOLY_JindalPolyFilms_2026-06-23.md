## Executive Summary

Jindal Poly Films (JPFL) is India's largest manufacturer of BOPP and BOPET flexible packaging films, operating through its wholly-owned subsidiary JPFL Films (Nashik — world's largest single-location BOPP/BOPET plant pre-fire). A catastrophic fire at the Nashik plant on May 21, 2025, destroyed approximately 70% of installed capacity (~695,000 TPA combined) and knocked out 90% of production at what was the company's core earnings engine. Revenue in H2 FY26 collapsed to ₹372–410 Cr per quarter from ₹1,100–1,400 Cr per quarter in FY25. The investment thesis is additionally impaired by an active Enforcement Directorate investigation for Rs 505 Cr FEMA violations (September 2025 raids), India's first ever NCLT-admitted class action lawsuit by minority shareholders alleging Rs 2,500 Cr of asset siphoning via promoter-linked transactions, CRISIL downgrading the parent two notches (AA- to A+), and structural governance red flags including RPTs, ICD-like infusions to subsidiaries, and management credibility concerns. The business cannot be analysed on normal-course financials until FY27–28 at the earliest; the governance trajectory makes any recovery thesis speculative. Fisher Point 15 (Management Integrity) is a potential veto trigger given active regulatory investigations and ongoing class action proceedings.

**Hard Stop:** Management integrity under investigation (ED/FEMA + class action alleging Rs 2,500 Cr siphoning) — Fisher Point 15 triggered. No investment recommended at any price until regulatory and legal outcomes are resolved.

## Business Primer

**Paragraph 1 — What the business does and how it makes money:**
Jindal Poly Films Limited is a holding company at the apex of the B.C. Jindal Group's packaging films business. Its primary operating subsidiary, JPFL Films Pvt. Ltd. (formerly the packaging films undertaking transferred via slump sale in FY23 for ~₹2,400 Cr), operates what was the world's largest single-location BOPP (Biaxially Oriented Polypropylene) and BOPET (Biaxially Oriented Polyethylene Terephthalate) film manufacturing complex in Nashik, Maharashtra, with installed capacities of 294,000 TPA (BOPP) and 173,000 TPA (BOPET) — totalling ~467,000 TPA. At the consolidated level, the group also operates non-woven fabrics (~14% of FY24 revenue, PP-based spunmelt/spunbond fabrics) and a self-adhesive labels division via subsidiary SMI Coated Products (~7% of revenue, 200+ products). Revenue is generated as Volume (MT) × Realization per MT, with the mix between commodity base films (lower margin) and specialty/value-added films (higher margin) being the key margin driver. The business is entirely B2B — customers are FMCG companies, food processors, pharmaceutical packagers, and flexible laminate converters.

**Paragraph 2 — What kind of business this is and what to watch:**
This is a capital-intensive, commodity-adjacent, cyclical manufacturer where pricing is determined by global supply-demand dynamics in BOPP/BOPET films, input costs are directly linked to polypropylene and PET polymer prices (themselves crude oil derivatives), and returns are heavily cycle-dependent. The business is asset-heavy with low capital-light characteristics — a new film line costs ₹200–400 Cr and takes 18–30 months to commission, making oversupply cycles long and painful. The single most important analytical lens here is the industry capital cycle: simultaneous capacity additions from multiple players (FY22–FY24 saw additions by SRF, Polyplex, Cosmo, and JPFL itself) created the FY23–FY24 trough (near-zero EBITDA margins). The May 2025 Nashik fire — the second fire at this plant in 2.5 years — has removed the company's entire operating capacity from the equation for FY26 and created a multi-year reconstruction uncertainty. The structural feature that colours the entire analysis is a toxic combination: (1) balance sheet with ₹4,420 Cr borrowings and ₹4,093 Cr in investments (treasury portfolio deployed to manage group liquidity), creating an unusual financial conglomerate-like structure; (2) active FEMA investigation and minority shareholder class action alleging systemic value siphoning through promoter-linked entities; and (3) a subsidiary fire that has created ₹2,000 Cr of insurance claims in dispute with six insurers. This is not a normal operating company in any recognisable sense during FY26.

## Module 1b — Sector Cycle: Tailwinds & Headwinds

### Secular vs. Cyclical Demand Decomposition

**BOPP/BOPET market structure:**
- India BOPP market: ~USD 5.78 billion (2025), growing 4–5% CAGR 2026–2031
- Secular demand growth: ~5–7% PA driven by flexible packaging penetration in FMCG, organised food retail, e-commerce, pharma
- Cyclical component FY23–FY25: Down-cycle driven by simultaneous capacity additions from SRF, Polyplex, Cosmo First, and JPFL itself (FY22 supercycle profits triggered aggressive capex)

**Secular baseline:** ~5–6% demand CAGR is durable. This should anchor DCF terminal growth — NOT the FY21–22 volume surge (which was cyclically amplified by COVID-related packaging demand + supply disruptions).

**Supply side (FY26 unusual dynamic):**
- The May 2025 Nashik fire removed ~467,000+ TPA JPFL Films capacity from the market (the world's largest single plant)
- This has materially tightened India supply-demand in BOPP/BOPET films through H2 FY26
- Near-term BENEFICIARIES: Polyplex, SRF packaging films, Cosmo First — all will see better pricing and utilisation
- NEW CAPACITY ADDITIONS COMING: SRF Indore 60,000 TPA (Jul 2027), Polyplex new lines — could re-create oversupply by FY28

**Input cost cycle (FY26):**
- Polypropylene and PET costs broadly stable in FY26 — mild tailwind vs. FY24 spike
- Crude oil prices moderated; polymer input benign
- China competition remains structural ceiling on global film pricing

**Geopolitical / Export risk:**
- JPFL had significant export exposure (~35–45% historically)
- Post-fire, exports are essentially nil from Nashik
- India-Pakistan April 2026 tensions caused some port/logistics disruption — transient

**Net FX Exposure (pre-fire):**
- Export revenue approximately 35–45% of total film revenue
- Net FX exposure positive (exporter) — INR depreciation tailwinds; INR appreciation headwind
- Post-fire, FX exposure is moot until production resumes

**Sector Cycle Verdict: MIXED**
- Industry level: mild TAILWIND (fire removed a major competitor's capacity, improving supply-demand)
- Company level: SEVERE HEADWIND (the company causing the tailwind for peers is itself devastated)
- Cyclical recovery that would normally drive re-rating has been obliterated by the fire event

**Downstream effect:** Do NOT embed FY25 recovery margins as permanent; the fire creates artificial FY26 trough that cannot be modelled with historical tools.

## Module 3 — Business Quality & Moat

**1. Supply/Cost Advantage:**
- Scale advantage exists at Nashik (world's largest single-location plant pre-fire)
- However, cost advantage is cyclically driven, not structural — in downturns, ALL players lose margin simultaneously
- No captive raw material source (unlike Coal India or Divi's)
- **Verdict: WEAK supply/cost advantage; scale is real but not moat-quality**

- Commodity base film: NONE — customers switch freely on price
- Specialty films: PARTIAL — 6–12 month qualification cycle provides switching cost; but Jindal's specialty mix was LOW (<20% historically vs. Cosmo First's 70%+)
- Capacitor film: WAS present historically (high-value BOPET for capacitors); current status unclear post-fire
- **Verdict: NARROW customer captivity; primarily in specialty niche; not dominant**

**3. Local Economies of Scale:**
- Nashik was the world's largest single-location plant — genuine scale
- But scale is destroyed by fire; reconstruction timeline unknown
- Even pre-fire, Polyplex (global scale: Thailand, Turkey, India) competed effectively
- **Verdict: Scale moat was real but is currently zero (fire); structural durability was always questionable given China competition**

**Moat Verdict: NONE (currently) / NARROW (at full operations + specialty mix increase)**

**Buffett Moat Test (pre-fire):** Could JPFL raise BOPP prices 10% without losing volume? **NO** — commodity film pricing is set by global supply-demand. Specialty films have some pricing power but they were <20% of revenue. FAIL.

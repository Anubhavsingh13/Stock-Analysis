## Executive Summary
Cochin Shipyard is India's largest shipyard by dry-dock capacity and the only defence yard with meaningful exposure to commercial international shipbuilding — a structural differentiation that gives it a TAM far larger than MAZDOCK or GRSE. The newly commissioned International Ship Repair Facility (ISRF) is a genuine capacity transformation: a world-class dry dock capable of handling Very Large Crude Carriers (VLCCs) and Suezmax vessels, with strategic MOUs already signed with Maersk, Drydocks World (UAE), and HD KSOE (South Korea). However, CSL faces two near-term headwinds that make the current 53.5× P/E (highest in the peer group) difficult to justify: (1) inventory days at 379 vs. 122 five years ago and negative CFO of ₹-1,234 Cr signal severe WC strain during ISRF ramp, and (2) the market has already priced the ISRF optionality without waiting to see ramp-up evidence. Wait for the first sign of WC normalisation and ISRF revenue contribution (expected Q2–Q3 FY27) before initiating.

## Business Primer
Cochin Shipyard (CSL), owned ~68% by the Government of India, operates India's largest shipyard complex in Kochi, Kerala. Unlike pure-defence peers MAZDOCK and GRSE, CSL operates across three distinct revenue streams: defence shipbuilding (Indian Navy/Coast Guard orders, ~50% of revenue), commercial shipbuilding (civilian vessels including offshore wind support ships for European customers, ~30%), and ship repair (domestic and international, ~20%). The newly commissioned ISRF adds a fourth stream: large-vessel international repair (capable of handling the world's largest tankers and container ships). Revenue flows are milestone-based for new builds and activity-based for repair. The business absolutely depends on: (a) continued MoD allocations for defence builds, (b) competitiveness of Indian labour/overhead costs vs. South Korean/Chinese commercial shipyards, and (c) successful ISRF utilisation ramp from 0% to 70%+ over FY27–28.

CSL is at a structural inflection: the ISRF represents a ₹2,000+ Cr capital investment that, at full utilisation, could generate ₹2,000–3,000 Cr additional annual revenue from international repair (ships in Arabian Sea routing now have an economic alternative to Singapore). The bear case is that ISRF ramp takes 3–4 years (not 2 years as optimists assume) and the heavy capex burden continues to suppress FCF and OCF. At 53.5× P/E with highest WC intensity and lowest ROCE (16%) in the peer group, CSL is the highest-risk, highest-optionality bet in the defence shipbuilding space.
